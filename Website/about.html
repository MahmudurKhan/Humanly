"My grandmother couldn’t walk well. She had diabetes. So whenever we’d go to the mall or something, we’d sit her down and she’d people watch and wait for us to finish. When we’d come back in half and hour or so, she’d have four to five white old ladies sitting with her, talking with her, and laughing. The thing is she couldn’t speak English! And here’s four or five white ladies laughing with her, hugging her. My grandmother only knew two phrases, “Thank you” and “I love you.” The old white ladies would say, “We can’t speak with her, but we can tell she’s so sweet.” And that was it. She was so sweet. She just had this ability to love no matter what. She just loved.

The secret was that she was illiterate. Yes, she was powerful and exceptionally sharp. But, she couldn’t read or write. But, that didn’t matter. Because she knew the language of love.

My grandmother died two years ago. Every other day, we remember the funny memories with her. People still pray for her all the time. They visit her grave. Her love was endless. She was love. She was a luminary…I miss her."

"I lived in a homogenous community when I was growing up. I was different. Probably the only brown kid in the school. I didn’t make much friends and no one really liked me. It didn’t really matter if I existed or not. And then in 3rd grade we moved to a very diverse neighborhood and I had a teacher named Ms. Wilson. She was in the civil rights movement. She’d say, “I walked alongside Dr. Martin Luther King and I went up to that ‘white’ water fountain and I drank from it!”

It was in the first few weeks of school that Ms. Wilson put on Dr. Martin Luther King’s “I Have a Dream” speech. When I heard Dr. King speak, it was the first time that I realized there was such a thing as equality. Such a thing as dignity and respect—that those weren’t privileges, but rights. My self-esteem changed. I was on top of the world.

We ended up moving back to my old neighborhood. Later that year, I heard that Ms. Wilson died from breast cancer. I didn’t really learn anything academic from her. But, I learned about life. Where else can you learn that in school?"

"That’s the thing: there are amazing people like Ms. Wilson, like my grandmother. They aren’t on the front page of the New York Times or anything. They’re not celebrities. But, they’re incredible. That’s why I created Luminaries. To give voice to those people. Those people that don’t have a voice, those people who embody what it means to be human. That’s what’s beautiful. And that’s what I wished to see more."
